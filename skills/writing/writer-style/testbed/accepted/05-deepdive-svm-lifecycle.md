# What actually happens to your transaction: the Solana lifecycle, end to end

Quick trivia question, and be honest about your first instinct: how many transactions are sitting in Solana's mempool right now, waiting their turn? Ten thousand? A couple million? Hold the number in your head for a second.

It's a trick question. There is no mempool. No global, public waiting room anywhere in the design, and there never was one. Every developer I've asked either invents a number or mumbles something about the RPC "holding" transactions somewhere, and both answers point at the same gap: we sign hundreds of transactions a week on this chain, we build products on it, and most of us have never traced a single one of those transactions below the `sendTransaction` call.

So let's trace one, start to finish, from the moment your client fires it off to the moment nothing can un-happen it. And rather than just naming the stages, which any docs page can do for you, I want to derive them. Every stage of this pipeline exists as the answer to a specific problem, and once you can see the problems, the stages stop reading like vocabulary to memorize and start reading like decisions somebody had to make.

## The thing you actually signed

Before the network touches anything, look at what your wallet produced. A Solana transaction is two parts: a list of signatures, and the message those signatures cover. The message has four pieces of its own: a header, the list of every account the transaction will touch, a recent blockhash, and the instructions themselves.

That account list is the strangest part if you came from another chain, so sit with it for a second. Every account any instruction will read or modify is declared up front, before anything executes, and each one is marked writable or read-only. A program cannot quietly reach into an account it didn't list. (If you've never actually inspected one of these, paste a transaction into the explorer's inspector tab before sending it sometime; it decodes the whole message for you, and staring at that raw account list taught me more about the runtime than any diagram ever did.)

Why so rigid? Because the entire machine downstream is built on one rule, and I want to name it now since we'll keep tripping over it for the rest of this piece: **declare, don't discover.** Solana pushes work onto you at signing time so the network never has to figure anything out at execution time. You declare which accounts you'll touch, so the runtime never discovers a conflict mid-flight. You stamp the message with a recent blockhash, so nobody downstream has to wonder how old it is. Hold onto that rule. Nearly every design choice between here and finality is the same rule in a different costume.

## A clock made of hashes

Start with the blockhash, since it's the first thing that kills transactions in production.

The recent blockhash your client fetched is a hash pulled from the Proof-of-History stream, and its job is to be a timestamp. A transaction is accepted while its blockhash sits within the last 150 blocks; the constant in the code is literally `MAX_PROCESSING_AGE = 150`, and it's why the `lastValidBlockHeight` you get back from the RPC equals the current block height plus 150. Blocks, not slots, and the distinction bites: slots can be skipped, while block height only counts blocks that actually happened. In wall-clock terms you get roughly 60–120 seconds of validity before the network stops accepting what you signed.

The natural question is why transactions should expire at all, and the answer falls out the moment you try to design replay protection yourself. The threat is simple. Someone grabs your signed transaction off the wire and submits it a second time; the signature is still valid, so what stops the transfer from executing twice?

The first naive fix is for validators to remember every signature ever executed and reject repeats, which works right up until you notice that the set grows forever and every incoming transaction now has to be checked against the entire history of the chain. The second fix is the per-account counter other chains use: every transaction carries a sequence number, and a replay fails because its number was already consumed. Bounded memory, replay-safe, genuinely reasonable — and also the reason transaction five can never land before transaction four. Your own transactions serialize behind each other, one stuck payment dams everything queued after it, and firing transactions from two machines at once means coordinating a counter between them.

The blockhash window is the third option. Prove the transaction is recent, and the network only has to remember the signatures inside a 150-block window to catch replays. Memory stays bounded, and nothing forces your transactions into single file behind each other. The price is the one you already pay in production: transactions can die on the shelf. That's the first of the three failure modes every Solana developer eventually memorizes, the expired blockhash, and it's worth internalizing that it isn't a bug or an RPC hiccup. It's the design charging you its listed price for a transaction that arrived late.

## The pipeline, in one breath

Once a transaction reaches a leader, it enters the TPU, the transaction processing unit, which runs five stages: Fetch → SigVerify → Banking stage → PoH → broadcast. Fetch and SigVerify are exactly what they sound like, ingestion plumbing and signature checking at the door. The interesting arguments live in how the transaction reaches the leader at all, in what the Banking stage decides, and in what happens after broadcast, so those are the stops we'll make.

## Why there is no mempool

Back to the trivia question, because now we can answer it properly. On most chains, a submitted transaction lands in a mempool: a shared waiting room, gossiped from node to node across the network, where it loiters until some block producer plucks it out. The waiting room exists to solve an unknown. Nobody knows who will produce the next block, so a pending transaction has to be everywhere at once, just in case.

Solana deletes the unknown instead of building the room. The leader schedule is computed an epoch in advance, meaning every node already knows which validator produces which upcoming slot, long before that slot arrives. So when your RPC node receives your transaction, it doesn't gossip it into a shared pool and hope; it forwards it directly to the current leader and the next scheduled ones. This forwarding is Gulf Stream, and notice what it actually is underneath the name: the schedule was declared ahead of time, so delivery turned into a routing problem, with nothing left to discover about where the transaction should go.

What you give up is any chain-level notion of "pending." If the leaders you forwarded to drop your transaction (blocks full, fee uncompetitive), it isn't queued anywhere. It isn't parked, it isn't waiting, it is simply gone, and the only recourse is to send it again. This is why every serious client ships retry logic, and why "my transaction vanished" is the most common confused bug report from people arriving off mempool chains — nothing vanished, because nothing was ever holding it.

## The Banking stage, where your parallelism gets decided

Your transaction made it to the leader. Now comes the stage where that account list from the top of the piece finally pays its rent, and since this is the heart of the machine, we're going to take our time here.

Start from the hardware. A modern server has dozens of cores, and a blockchain that executes transactions one at a time is using exactly one of them, no matter how much you spend on the box. If two transactions touch completely unrelated accounts, say your token transfer and a stranger's NFT listing, there is no physical reason one should wait for the other. The naive fix, run everything at once and sort it out later, dies immediately: two transactions writing the same account concurrently is a double-spend generator. The next fix up, start executing optimistically and abort when a conflict appears, is a real technique and databases use it every day, but you pay full price for the work you throw away, and on a chain where blockspace is metered, wasted execution is everybody's cost, not just yours.

Look at what the clean solution requires: knowing each transaction's read set and write set *before* running it. Which is exactly the thing Solana made you declare when you signed. This is declare-don't-discover doing its heaviest lifting. Because every arriving transaction carries its complete account list, the Banking stage scheduler can sort work into lanes before executing a single instruction. The mechanism is account locks: a transaction takes an exclusive lock on every account it marked writable, and a shared lock on every account it marked read-only. Two transactions with disjoint account lists hold no locks in common and run in parallel on separate executor threads; the runtime that pulls this off is Sealevel. Two transactions that both write the same account collide on the exclusive lock and get serialized, one behind the other, exactly like the single-file chains we were trying to escape. But only those two. Everyone else keeps flowing.

That "only those two" carries more consequence than it looks like it should. It means congestion on Solana is local, not global. When a hyped mint has half the internet hammering one writable account, the transactions touching that account fight viciously over priority while your unrelated transfer strolls past the whole mess. This is where local fee markets come from, and they're less a feature someone wrote than a consequence that falls out of the locking scheme: the fee market forms per contended account, not chain-wide.

It also hands you the other two failure modes from the list of three. Failure mode two: your fee was too low for a contended writable account. You weren't priced out of the chain, you were priced out of one neighborhood of it, which is why the fix is a priority fee scoped to that moment and not despair. Failure mode three is the sneaky one, and I'd argue the most expensive, because it hides in architecture rather than in operations: account-lock contention serializing what you assumed was parallel. Route every instruction in your program through one global state account marked writable, and you have built a single-file queue with extra steps. The scheduler will dutifully line your users up behind each other no matter how many cores sit idle, and no fee in the world unblocks a lock that another transaction is holding. On paper you had parallelism, but the lock table says otherwise — when you lay out your program's accounts you're designing its concurrency, whether you meant to or not.

The lock system, though, is only as smart as your declarations. Mark an account writable that you only ever read, and you serialize against everyone genuinely writing it, for zero benefit; the runtime takes your declaration at its word and never checks your intent. Half the contention I've helped people untangle wasn't the network being congested, it was their own transaction over-claiming locks it didn't need.

Then there's what actually runs once a transaction holds its locks. Your instructions execute as SBF bytecode, an eBPF-derived format, inside the Solana Virtual Machine, and every operation is metered in compute units. A single transaction gets a ceiling of 1.4M CU. The block has ceilings of its own, and those have been raised repeatedly through governance, 48M to 50M to 60M CU through 2025, each bump shipped as a SIMD.

## Getting the block out of the building

While executor threads chew through transactions, the leader stamps the results into the Proof-of-History stream as entries, and the finished work has to reach everyone else. The obvious distribution scheme, where the leader uploads its block to every validator directly, fails on plain arithmetic: one node's uplink multiplied by every other validator on the network becomes the bottleneck for the entire chain, and the leader's slot turns into a bandwidth test. So the block travels as erasure-coded shreds through Turbine, a stake-weighted fan-out tree. The leader sends shreds to a first layer of nodes, each of those forwards to its own set below, and the load spreads so that no single node ever uploads the block to everyone. The erasure coding is what lets the tree survive real networks, since a validator can reconstruct the full block even when some of the shreds never arrive.

## Three different words for "done"

Your transaction executed and the block shipped. What's left is the word everyone argues about: final.

Every other validator replays the block (the stage is literally named Replay) and then votes on it. The votes are themselves transactions, riding the exact same lifecycle your transfer just did, blockhash and all.

The commitment levels are three different answers to how sure we are, and they are not interchangeable. `processed` means the leader's bank saw your transaction: one machine's opinion, useful for a spinner and not much else. `confirmed` means optimistic confirmation: at least 2/3 of stake has voted on the block containing it. `finalized` means the block is rooted, with 31+ confirmed blocks built on top of it.

The gap between those words deserves respect, and I learned mine the embarrassing way. My team once spent two hours debugging a failing deploy, rewriting retry logic, swapping RPC endpoints, quietly doubting our own keypairs, before discovering the problem was neither our code nor our config: we had managed to actually break the testnet itself. Two hours staring at statuses that were never going to advance, because the chain underneath them had stopped behaving like a chain. I keep the story around because it makes commitment concrete. These levels are claims about validator behavior, and when the validators themselves are wedged, no client-side setting rescues you.

For real rollback risk, the numbers make the argument better than adjectives can. An optimistically confirmed block can only be rolled back if at least 1/3 of stake is willing to be slashable for it. Real stake burned, not a free reorg. So `confirmed` is an economic guarantee rather than a mathematical one, and the practical guidance falls out of what you're protecting. For a payments UI, confirmed is what your users will experience as done. For a bridge withdrawal, or anything irreversible on the far side, wait for finalized and accept the extra time. And next time a dapp parks you on a spinner, you know the useful question to ask: which of the three words is it actually waiting for?

## The trace, run backwards

Now run the whole thing in reverse, because backwards is where it stops being a list. Finality is votes; votes are transactions; blocks travel as shreds through a stake-weighted tree because one uplink can't feed a network; the scheduler could lane transactions only because every one of them declared its accounts; the transaction found its leader without any mempool because the schedule was declared an epoch ahead; and the blockhash bounded what the network ever has to remember about the past. Declare, don't discover, at every single layer.

ps: if you ever need a transaction signed now but submitted later than the 150-block window allows (a hardware wallet in a drawer, a multisig waiting on someone's weekend), durable nonces exist for exactly that: offline and delayed signing without the expiry clock running against you.
