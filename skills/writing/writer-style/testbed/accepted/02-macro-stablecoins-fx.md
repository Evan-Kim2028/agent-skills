# Why stablecoins are eating emerging-market FX

Maybe it's a peso unraveling in Buenos Aires, maybe an election cycle in Brasília, or maybe nothing dramatic at all, just an ordinary decade in which no single headline explains why your salary buys fewer dollars every year — either way the story ends the same for anyone earning in an emerging-market currency: you did everything right, you saved, and the number in your account held steady while the value underneath it drained out. Stablecoin adoption is strongest exactly where local currencies are weakest, and I want to walk you through why with actual figures, because once you see the demand side clearly, the $250 billion-plus of USDT and USDC in circulation (2025 reporting, give or take) stops reading like a crypto statistic and starts reading like an FX position held by millions of people who never once thought of themselves as traders.

Here's the route, so you know what you're signing up for. Money has two jobs, and I'm going to keep them separated the whole way down: getting paid, which means moving value from someone else's account into yours, and staying paid, which means keeping that value intact while it waits between your paycheck and your rent. Brazil solved the first job so completely that half the pitches I hear are people solving it again by accident; the second job is where the entire stablecoin story actually lives, and confusing the two is, in my experience, how smart teams end up building products nobody in the region asked for.

## Getting paid was solved in November 2020

If you're reading this from outside Brazil, you need one piece of context first, because it breaks most visitors' priors: Brazilians do not need crypto for payments. PIX, the central bank's own instant-payment rail, launched in November 2020, free to use and settled in seconds, and it won so thoroughly that the bar tab, the street vendor, the dentist and your grandmother all run on it now. And a confession, since it's cheaper than you learning this yourself: early on I made the crypto-fixes-payments pitch to Brazilian audiences more than once, with slides, with conviction, and the polite silence in those rooms was an audience too kind to point out that the central bank had already shipped the thing I was describing, for free, without a token. I've been on the wrong side of this one.

So hold that context next to the strangest number in this piece. The BCB itself has said, through Campos Neto's 2024 statements, that roughly 90% of Brazilian crypto flows are stablecoins. Sit with that for a second: in the country with arguably the best domestic payment rail on the planet, where moving money is about as solved as it gets anywhere, nine-tenths of crypto volume is people buying tokenized dollars. If stablecoins were competing with PIX, Brazil should be the market where they matter least, and instead it's the one where they dominate, which tells you the demand was never really about payments: PIX moves reais, brilliantly, and for a growing number of Brazilians the currency itself is the thing they're trying to get some distance from.

## Staying paid is the half nobody shipped

The real traded roughly between 4.8 and 6.2 to the dollar across 2020-2025, and I'd rather you feel that range than read it, so run it against your own numbers: whatever you pay in rent, whatever your laptop cost, whatever share of your groceries has a dollar hiding somewhere in its supply chain, all of it repriced depending on which year your contract happened to renew, while your salary stayed denominated in the thing doing the moving. Nobody experiences that as a chart. You experience it at the checkout, in the import markup on a phone, in a tuition invoice that grew while your paycheck didn't, and you experience it without ever having made a single bad financial decision.

Argentina is the same mechanism with the safety off. The peso lost more than 90% of its dollar value across 2019-2024, and Argentines responded by making USDT a default savings vehicle without anyone needing to onboard them, because when your currency loses ninety-plus percent in five years you do not require a whitepaper or a wallet tutorial, you require dollars, and the informal dollar market that already existed on every corner simply grew a digital leg and kept doing what it had always done.

Notice which half of the money problem this is, because it decides what fixes it. A depreciating currency moves through payment rails just fine, arguably faster, since nobody wants to be the one still holding it at the end of the month; the failure lives in the sitting, in the stretch between getting paid and spending, and that stretch is exactly where a tokenized dollar earns its keep. Strip the branding and a stablecoin is the oldest financial product in the world, a dollar that sits still, held from a phone, no US bank account required, no minimum balance, no appointment with a manager, available to the exact population the dollar banking system never bothered to onboard — and if that sounds unambitious to you, price how many people want precisely that and cannot get it any other way.

## The corridor toll, and what a transfer actually cost me

Cross a border and the two jobs collide. Remittance inflows to Latin America and the Caribbean run on the order of $150 billion a year, per World Bank reporting from the 2023-2024 era, and the global average cost of a traditional remittance corridor sits near 6%, so do the napkin math with me on a realistic case: a worker abroad sending $300 home each month loses about $18 of it to fees before it lands, month after month, compounding to roughly $216 a year handed to intermediaries for what is, mechanically, an update to two databases and a compliance checklist. The people paying that toll are, almost by definition, the people with the least room in the budget for it.

Here's my receipt, because I don't quote fee comparisons I haven't run with my own money. Back in August 2020, long before Solana was part of my daily stack, I benchmarked exactly this trade: I moved BRZ, a real-pegged stablecoin, four times across the Syscoin bridge and four times as a plain ERC-20 on Ethereum, same wallets, same week, while gas was running 200-260 gwei. The ERC-20 transfers cost me $5.53 to $5.55 each. The bridge transfers cost $0.00000582. Six orders of magnitude. The honest catch came attached, because it always does: the bridge proofs took about three hours to finalize, so I paid for the discount in waiting. On Solana today that particular trade barely exists anymore, a stablecoin transfer settles in seconds and the base fee is 5,000 lamports per signature, a fraction of a cent, small enough that writing it in dollars needs notation nobody uses at a grocery store.

Now the trade-off I owe you before you get carried away: the transfer is the cheap leg. Getting from reais or pesos into stablecoins and back out, the on-ramp and the off-ramp, is still where the real friction and the real fees live, and anyone quoting you "the whole corridor costs a fraction of a cent" is pricing the one leg that got fixed and staying quiet about the two that didn't. What changed is which leg is expensive: the middle of the corridor used to be the toll; now the middle is nearly free and the ends are a business problem rather than a physics problem, and business problems have a habit of attracting competitors.

## The seam is where you build

So where does that leave you, the person deciding what to build? Put the two stories side by side, because the gap between them is the actual opportunity here. Domestic payments: solved, by the state, for free — don't compete with that. Dollar savings and cross-border settlement: unsolved by anything local, served today by stablecoins as raw infrastructure with rough edges exactly where ordinary users stand. Sitting on the seam between those two facts is a product traditional retail banking in these markets has flatly never offered: FX-hedged, dollar-denominated yield, accessible from a phone. Your local bank will happily sell you a savings product in the currency that spent the decade leaking; a dollar account with real yield was reserved for corporates and for people who already had a private banker's phone number. At Superteam Brazil I get a front-row seat to what actually gets built here, and a noticeable share of the builders coming through are working this exact seam, payroll that lands in USDC, ramp infrastructure attacking the expensive ends of the corridor, savings products designed for people who think in grocery bills rather than basis points, and not one of them is spending a quarter building a better PIX, because they live here, they settle their own bar tabs on the original, and they know exactly which half of the problem is still open.

## What would prove me wrong

A macro read without a failure condition is just vibes with citations, so here are mine, concretely. Watch the real: if BRL/USD settles into a tight band and stays there for years, the savings-hedge demand thins out on its own and this piece ages badly. Watch the banks: the day a Brazilian retail bank offers an ordinary customer an insured dollar account with real yield inside an app, most of this thesis becomes their product launch instead of our opportunity. And watch the flow number: if the BCB's stablecoin share falls hard while the currency stays weak, then I misread the demand and the flows were casino churn after all. None of the three has moved that way in the five years I've been watching — check the next BCB release against this paragraph.
